If you only read one page

What to do first

When life feels uncertain, do not try to fix everything. Root yourself. Then take one clear step.

1
Calm your body.
Three minutes of slow breathing can change the quality of your next decision.
2
Know your place.
Learn your bioregion, seasons, rainfall, fire risks, water sources, local growers and neighbours.
3
Grow something.
Start with herbs, greens, potatoes, beans, fruit trees, compost and one protected growing bed.
4
Build a simple home buffer.
Food you already eat, water, first aid, light, warmth, basic tools, important documents.
5
Find your people.
Community is resilience. Share meals, seeds, tools, skills and honest conversations.

Hope is not passive. Hope becomes real when it turns into one small act of care.

Regenerative interbeing

Thriving begins when we remember that we are not separate from the web of life.

We are shaped by soil, water, food, breath, sunlight, relationships, culture and place. When we care for ourselves, we become more capable of caring for others. When we care for land, the land nourishes us in return.

This is what we call regenerative interbeing: living in ways that help ourselves, our communities and the living world flourish together.

For Benjy, this understanding arrived through documentaries that opened the heart, meditation in nature, yoga, tree planting, ocean experiences, community and the growing willingness to say: Yes. I am ready to do the work.

The balance

Feeling the oneness of life can awaken deep care. It can also open us to grief. The path is not to shut down — and not to burn out.

Feel deeply. Act wisely. Rest often. Walk together.

Begin with your nervous system

A regulated body is one of the greatest forms of preparedness.

When the nervous system is constantly activated, everything feels urgent. When the body softens, we can listen, plan, repair, collaborate and respond more wisely.

Grounding practice

Stand barefoot if possible. Feel your feet. Look around slowly. Name five things you can see, four things you can feel, three things you can hear, two things you can smell, and one thing you appreciate.

Shake and release

Shake your hands, arms, shoulders and legs for 60–90 seconds. Then pause. Feel the body. Let the breath settle. Animals naturally discharge stress through movement; we can remember this too.

Breath is a doorway

Breathwork for calm, energy and presence

Start gently. Strong breath practices can be powerful, but they are not suitable for everyone.

Safety note: If you are pregnant, have cardiovascular concerns, epilepsy, panic disorder, serious mental health concerns, high blood pressure, or you feel dizzy, practise gently and seek qualified guidance. Do not practise intense breathing while driving, in water, or anywhere fainting would be dangerous.

1. Heart-coherent breathing

Place a hand on your heart. Inhale gently for about 5–6 seconds. Exhale for about 5–6 seconds. Bring to mind gratitude, appreciation or love. Continue for 3–5 minutes.

This slow rhythm is commonly used to support heart-rate variability and a calmer state. Keep it soft. No forcing.

2. Longer exhale breathing

Inhale for 4 seconds. Exhale for 6–8 seconds. Repeat for 2–5 minutes. A longer exhale can gently invite the body toward relaxation.

3. Belly breathing

Lie down or sit comfortably. Let the belly expand on the inhale and soften on the exhale. Imagine the breath massaging the nervous system from inside.

4. Alternate nostril breathing

A gentle yogic practice for balance. Close one nostril, inhale. Switch sides, exhale. Continue slowly. Keep the breath smooth and easy.

5. Kapalabhati / fire breath

Short, active exhales through the nose with passive inhales. Traditionally used as an energising yogic breath. Start with 15–30 gentle pumps, then rest and breathe normally. Learn from an experienced teacher if new.

6. Energise, hold, release

For experienced practitioners only: a short round of energising breath, a comfortable pause, gently drawing awareness upward through the body, then full relaxation and release. Keep it safe, short and respectful of your body.

More intensive connected or holotropic-style breathwork can be deeply powerful, but is best practised with trained facilitators and proper support.

Nature is medicine

Walk. Sit under trees. Touch soil. Watch birds. Put your phone away for a while. Nature reminds the body of rhythms older than stress.

  • Morning sunlight when possible.
  • Daily walks without headphones sometimes.
  • Gardening, even for ten minutes.
  • Water, ocean, rivers, rain, mist.
  • Quiet time with trees.

Regeneration includes rest

Forests have seasons. Fields lie fallow. Humans need recovery too. Rest is not laziness. Rest allows care to continue.

Food, nutrients and living resilience

Foundations first: sleep, sunlight, movement, whole foods, clean water, community and nervous-system care.

Grow staples

Potatoes, sweet potatoes, beans, pumpkins, onions, garlic, greens, herbs, fruit trees, nuts where suitable, and climate-appropriate perennials.

Build soil

Compost, mulch, manure where safe, worm castings, leaves, wood chips, biochar when charged, chop-and-drop and living roots.

Preserve abundance

Ferment, dry, pickle, freeze, store seeds, share surplus and learn simple seasonal cooking.

Nutrients many people explore

This is educational, not medical advice. Needs differ. Speak with a qualified health professional if pregnant, on medication, managing thyroid/heart/kidney conditions, or unsure.

Vitamin D3 + K2

Often discussed where sunlight exposure is low. Testing can help guide whether supplementation is appropriate.

Magnesium bisglycinate

Commonly used for relaxation and muscle support. Food sources include nuts, seeds, legumes, cacao and leafy greens.

Omega-3 / algae oil

Algae oil can provide DHA/EPA for people avoiding fish. Food foundations still matter.

Iodine

Essential for thyroid hormone production, but too much can be harmful. Be especially careful with kelp or high-dose products.

Minerals and sea salt

Mineral balance matters, especially with sweating, heat and physical work. Avoid excessive salt if medically advised.

Lion's mane mushroom

Many people explore fruiting-body and mycelium extracts. Research is still developing; choose quality and listen to your body.

Conscious preparedness

Preparedness is not fear. It is love made practical.

  • Keep a sensible buffer of foods you already eat.
  • Store water appropriate to your situation.
  • Have first aid basics and know how to use them.
  • Keep light, batteries, blankets, basic tools and important documents accessible.
  • Learn simple natural remedies and when professional care is needed.
  • Know neighbours, local growers, local risks and community support networks.

Know your bioregion

Learn your rainfall, fire season, winds, soils, local trees, edible plants, water flows, community gardens, farmers' markets and elders who know the place.

Living well with technology

Technology can serve life when we use it consciously.

For EMF concerns, we recommend simple low-risk habits first: distance, airplane mode when practical, not sleeping with the phone next to your head, wired connections when convenient, screen-free time, outdoor time and fewer unnecessary notifications.

Some people explore EMF shielding products. Choose carefully, look for independent testing, and avoid products that make extreme health promises. Current mainstream health agencies generally do not find consistent evidence of harm from radiofrequency exposure below established limits, while practical exposure-reduction habits can still be part of a calm digital lifestyle.

One action today

Tonight, charge your phone away from your bed or use airplane mode if appropriate.

Community is resilience

No one thrives alone. Know your neighbours. Share meals. Exchange seeds. Learn skills together. Start small circles of trust, laughter, honesty and practical care.

In changing times, the most important infrastructure may be human relationship.

Authentic connection is preparedness. A shared meal can be as important as a stocked shelf.

A seven-day reset

Day 1

Breathe for five minutes and write what matters most.

Day 2

Walk your neighbourhood and notice water, food, trees and people.

Day 3

Plant one herb or prepare one bed.

Day 4

Create a simple food and water buffer.

Day 5

Call someone. Build relationship before crisis.

Day 6

Choose one skill to learn this month.

Day 7

Rest, reflect and celebrate one small victory.
